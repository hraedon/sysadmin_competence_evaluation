The CAB is not functioning as a risk management mechanism. It is functioning as an approval-and-accountability-diffusion mechanism, which is a real organizational function but not risk reduction.

The diagnosis comes from three data points in the report that, taken together, are unambiguous:

Data point 1: 0.38% rejection/modification rate
Three changes rejected or substantially modified out of 780 reviewed. This rate is not interpretable as positive evidence without outcome data. The charitable interpretation ("the team writes excellent plans") cannot be distinguished from the approval-theater interpretation ("the board is rubber-stamping") without comparing the board's risk assessments to what actually happened. The report makes that comparison possible — and it is not made.

Data point 2: 6.2% deviation rate on executed changes, zero reviewed by CAB
47 changes required significant deviation from the approved plan. These are the changes where the pre-change plan was wrong in a recoverable way. The executor encountered something the plan didn't anticipate and adapted in real time. From a risk management learning perspective, these are the most valuable data points available: they represent identified, recoverable planning failures. Every one of them is an unexamined calibration opportunity for the board.

The 18 incidents (2.4%) are the more visible failure cases, and they presumably generate post-mortems through the incident management process. But post-mortems focus on what went wrong during execution. They do not evaluate what was wrong with the plan, the approval process, or the risk assessment. That evaluation requires the CAB to review the change, not the incident. Without it, the change management process and the incident management process are disconnected feedback loops that cannot learn from each other.

Data point 3: The board has never reviewed its own outcomes
Zero outcome reviews in 52 weeks of meetings. This is the mechanism that makes the near-zero rejection rate uninterpretable as a positive signal. A risk review process that never compares its risk assessments to outcomes has no means of knowing whether its assessments are accurate. The board cannot know whether the 740 changes approved as submitted were actually well-planned, or which of the 47 deviations could have been anticipated by a more rigorous review. The risk assessment criteria are unevaluated hypotheses that have been in use for 12 months without any ground truth to check them against.

The organizational dynamic that produces this state: high volume (15 changes per meeting, 87 minutes → ~6 minutes per change) creates time pressure. Time pressure normalizes the path of least resistance, which is approval. The norm of approval becomes established through repetition. The norm of not reviewing outcomes is then consistent with the norm of approval — reviewing outcomes would generate data that might challenge the approval norm. The process has self-organized to minimize review friction, which is the opposite of its stated function.

This is not a description of individual failures. The 7 board members are presumably competent at their jobs. The process has created incentives and norms that are inconsistent with genuine risk management, and the people in the process have responded to those incentives rationally.

Functional changes that would restore risk management:

1. Standing agenda item: Review of 2 approved changes from the prior 30 days (random sample, not just recent ones). Goal: what was the outcome? Did it go as planned? If not, why not? This takes 15 minutes per meeting and generates calibration data that the board currently has none of. After 6 months of this, the board's risk assessments will be visibly better because they will be based on evidence rather than intuition.

2. Mandatory deviation review: Any change that required significant deviation from the approved plan generates a brief written report to the next CAB meeting. The report answers: what deviated, what assumption in the plan was wrong, and what the review process could have surfaced. This is the feedback loop for the 47 deviations currently invisible to the board.

3. Rejection rate target with calibration: Set a target rejection/modification rate of 10-15% — not as a goal in itself, but as a diagnostic. If the rate is consistently near zero, something is wrong with the review. If it is consistently above 20%, something is wrong with the submission quality. Calibrate the target against outcome data after 6 months of review tracking.

Cosmetic changes that would not restore function:

- Reducing changes per meeting: Same dynamics, fewer changes reviewed. The 6 minutes per change becomes 9 minutes without changing the approval norm or adding feedback.
- More documentation requirements: More documentation to approve without reviewing what happens after approval.
- Adding board members: Broader accountability diffusion; same absence of feedback loops.

On accountability diffusion: the board currently serves a real organizational function. When a change produces an incident, the CAB approval record demonstrates that the change was reviewed by the Infrastructure Lead, Network Lead, Security Lead, DBA Lead, Operations Manager, and IT Director. Organizational fault is distributed across seven people. This protects individuals and provides organizational cover. This is not nothing — distributed accountability is sometimes the appropriate organizational response to uncertainty. But it should be explicitly recognized as what the CAB is providing, not confused with risk reduction. The infrastructure manager asked whether the CAB is functioning as a risk management mechanism. The answer is no. The answer to whether it is serving any organizational function is yes, but a different one.
