This dispute is not a performance failure of either team. It is a failure of the project specification to define the temporal scope of a security requirement, and both parties are paying the price for that gap.

David's team had a coherent and defensible interpretation: the spec required servers to be "deployed in a security-baseline-compliant state," and their provisioning process produces exactly that — a deployment-time snapshot documented with scan results. From an Infrastructure perspective, "apply the baseline" is a one-time configuration action, not an enrollment event. Their sign-off documentation shows clean compliance at handover, which is the deliverable they understood themselves to owe.

Priya's team had an equally coherent interpretation: in a security operations context, "compliant" is not a moment-in-time property but an ongoing state. Her tooling is built to measure continuous compliance, her team's policies define it that way, and she has never encountered a provisioning project where point-in-time compliance was considered sufficient. A server that drifts out of compliance hasn't become non-compliant — from her model, it was always going to become non-compliant once patches were released, and enrollment in monitoring is what prevents that.

Neither team stated their interpretation before the work began because neither recognized there was an interpretation to state. Each model was internally consistent enough that the other reading simply didn't surface. Infrastructure had no reason to ask "do you mean ongoing compliance?" because their reading of the spec was unambiguous to them. Security had no reason to clarify "we mean continuous compliance" because they assumed that was the only reasonable meaning. The gap was invisible to both parties until Priya's platform flagged drift.

The practical consequence is also unresolved: the spec assigns baseline application to Infrastructure but says nothing about who enrolls new servers in continuous monitoring systems at provisioning time, or who owns drift remediation going forward. That ownership question needs to be answered regardless of how the spec dispute is resolved — and it is likely to produce the same argument again on the next provisioning project if it isn't addressed explicitly.
