This dispute is not a performance failure of either team. It is a failure of the project specification to define the temporal scope of a security requirement — and the mechanism of failure is traceable to a specific phrase.

David's team had a coherent and defensible interpretation: "deployed in a security-baseline-compliant state" describes a state at deployment, and their provisioning process produces exactly that. From an Infrastructure perspective, "apply the baseline" is a one-time configuration action. Their sign-off documentation shows clean compliance at handover, which is the deliverable they understood themselves to owe.

Priya's team had an equally coherent interpretation: in a security operations context, "compliant" is a continuous property, not a snapshot. Her tooling measures ongoing state, her policies define baseline compliance as continuous, and she has no operational model in which point-in-time compliance satisfies a security requirement.

Neither team surfaced the divergence before delivery because each operated within an internally consistent model that gave them no reason to question their reading. David's model of "apply and document" and Priya's model of "enroll and monitor" are both standard practice — they just describe different phases of what a complete provisioning process should look like. The conflict was invisible until the gap was discovered post-delivery.

The load-bearing phrase is "at provisioning time." By anchoring the compliance obligation to a specific moment, the spec made Infrastructure's point-in-time reading the natural reading of the sentence. A continuous-compliance requirement does not include a temporal anchor. It uses "must maintain" or "must remain compliant" — language that describes an ongoing state rather than a moment of arrival. The phrase "at provisioning time" appeared to scope Infrastructure's responsibility (which it does), but it simultaneously anchored "compliant state" to that same moment, which made the continuous reading invisible in the text even though it was Priya's intended meaning.

The spec should have read something like: "All servers provisioned under this project must be enrolled in the corporate compliance monitoring platform (SCCM / Defender for Endpoint) at provisioning time and must maintain continuous baseline compliance throughout their operational lifetime. The Infrastructure team is responsible for provisioning and initial monitoring enrollment. Security Operations is responsible for ongoing compliance monitoring, drift alerting, and exception management."

This version separates the provisioning action (Infrastructure's scope) from the ongoing compliance state (a shared responsibility with explicit ownership), names the specific tooling so "monitoring" is not ambiguous, and uses "must maintain" to make clear the requirement is continuous. It closes both the temporal ambiguity and the enrollment ownership gap that the original spec left unaddressed.
