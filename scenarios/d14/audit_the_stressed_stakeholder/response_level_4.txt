Mike’s communication during this incident represents a failure of "Theory of Mind"—he failed to model Sarah’s needs or the business context, leading to a breakdown in trust that is more damaging than the technical outage itself.

Key Failures:
1. Jargon Overload: Mike used internal technical details ("deadlock on SQL," "IOPS spike") as a shield. Sarah doesn't need to know about LUNs; she needs to know the system is unusable and what IT is doing to restore it. 
2. Lack of Operational Empathy: Sarah gave Mike the business impact (12 trucks waiting). Mike’s failure to acknowledge this pain signals to the business that IT is disconnected from the company’s core mission.
3. Adversarial Posture: Treating a stakeholder as a distraction is a professional failure. Mike failed to realize that Sarah’s "pinging" is a symptom of his own failure to provide a reliable update cadence.

Remediation and Corrective Approach:
Mike should have used a "Translate, Empathize, Commit" pattern:
- Translate: "We've identified a storage issue slowing down the database. We are expanding capacity now to restore service."
- Empathize: "I understand we have trucks waiting and this is a high-priority situation. We are moving as fast as possible."
- Commit: "I can't give an exact time yet, but conservatively, expect at least 30 minutes. I will send you a proactive update in 15 minutes, even if the status hasn't changed."

By setting a 15-minute cadence, Mike would have stopped Sarah from pinging him, managed the VP's expectations, and maintained professional trust during a crisis. His current approach damages IT’s reputation and creates an 'us vs. them' dynamic that will persist long after the database is fixed.
